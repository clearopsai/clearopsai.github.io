The five patterns we see repeatedly

These are not hypothetical. They appear in nearly every government IT firm between $30M and $50M in revenue with 100 to 250 employees.

1. The Monday Morning Assembly Line

Someone on the ops team spends Friday afternoon or Monday morning pulling data from the CRM, the project management tool, the finance system, and HR into a single leadership report. This takes 3 to 5 hours every week and the data is already stale by the time it reaches the executive team.

Typical cost: $28K to $42K/year in senior staff time

2. Contract Performance Reporting by Spreadsheet

Program managers track contract deliverables, burn rates, and milestones in individual spreadsheets. Rolling these up into a portfolio view for leadership requires manual aggregation from 5 to 15 separate trackers, depending on the number of active contracts.

Typical cost: $18K to $30K/year plus delayed decision-making on underperforming contracts

3. Compliance Tracking Across Multiple Frameworks

NIST, FISMA, CMMC, and agency-specific requirements are tracked in disconnected documents. When audit season arrives, assembling evidence takes weeks instead of hours because there is no centralized compliance dashboard.

Typical cost: 2 to 4 weeks of audit prep time that could be reduced to 2 to 3 days

4. Utilization Rates Are a Black Box

Leadership cannot answer "what is our utilization rate across the firm?" without a multi-day exercise. Billable vs. non-billable hours are tracked at the individual level but never rolled up automatically. This leads to overstaffing on some contracts and understaffing on others.

Typical cost: 8% to 15% revenue leakage from suboptimal resource allocation

5. The Hiring and Onboarding Gap

Fast-growing firms add headcount faster than they can update their internal systems. New hires take 30 to 60 days longer than necessary to reach full productivity because onboarding workflows, system access provisioning, and clearance processing are not connected.

Typical cost: $8K to $15K per new hire in delayed productivity

What the fix typically looks like

The firms that solve this do not rip out their tools. They build a visibility layer on top of what already exists.

Phase 1: Map the current state (1 to 2 weeks)

Document every tool, data flow, and manual process. Identify the 3 to 5 highest cost bottlenecks. Produce a prioritized action roadmap with estimated ROI for each fix.

Phase 2: Connect and automate (3 to 6 weeks)

Integrate the systems that matter most. Build automated dashboards for leadership reporting, contract performance, and utilization tracking. Eliminate the Monday morning assembly line.

Phase 3: Maintain and evolve (ongoing)

As the firm grows and adds contracts, the visibility layer grows with it. Monthly monitoring ensures nothing breaks and new tools get integrated as they are adopted.
